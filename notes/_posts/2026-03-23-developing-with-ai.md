title: "Using LLMs critically as a new grad"
date: 2026-03-23
---

Recently, I've been feeling increasing pressure to use an LLM agent for my projects. I don't think I'm alone in this. If anything, I'm probably a bit late to it because I've been resisting fully committing to using LLMs in my projects for a number of reasons. There are some great articles and videos exploring the changes AI is introducing to our lives, like [this](https://medium.com/@bchiang7/welcome-to-the-ai-parade-765f79b00c30) article from Brittany Chiang, and [this](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8MLbOulrLA0&t=1383s) video by Hank Green. I'm not sure I'll add anything particularly original to the conversation, but I wanted to explore my experience with LLMs as a new graduate who started learning to code in late 2022, right before ChatGPT was released.

I had been meaning to learn programming for several years before I finally started. I had this vague feeling that I would enjoy programming. Maths was my best subject in school and university, and I had often wished I had pursued something that involved the kind of structure and pattern recognition that maths involved. I started learning through freeCodeCamp, where I learnt HTML and CSS before moving onto JavaScript, where it started to hook me. I struggled through understanding for loops and array mutations and worked on problems like converting a number to Roman numerals.

One of my earlier projects that was kicked off by a freeCodeCamp certification task was a Pomodoro timer. I remember finding someone's blog where they explained how to clear intervals in React and why it was important, and finding another blog article explaining how to pass props to child components. It was always satisfying to discover something someone had written that solved exactly the problem I was trying to solve. For some problems, I knew that I could Google a solution, but I would avoid doing that and instead enjoyed getting stuck in the nitty-gritty detail and figuring it out myself, piece by piece.

While I was learning, LLMs were becoming more and more prevalent. Now, in 2026, it seems like suddenly they are taking over the exact tasks that I enjoyed. I know now that I can just ask an LLM and it can give me a detailed answer to most of my coding problems and build a Pomodoro timer for me within seconds. That should be exciting and liberating, and it is impressive. But it's also terrifying how well AI agents can do the exact thing I enjoy doing, and the exact tasks that would probably be assigned to a junior.

Recently, my partner and I started building a home dashboard to help with managing our day-to-day household tasks. This isn't necessarily a passion project for either one of us; it was more of a functional project that we wanted to get up and running as soon as we could so that we could start using it. So to speed things up, we used Claude to create the starter template code that we could build upon. Because Claude knew the codebase we were working with, I started leaning on it more heavily to implement new modules to add to the dashboard. While I still made sure to understand and customise the code Claude was suggesting, when I felt impatient to get something working so I could use it, it became easier to rely on Claude to create components and to skim them for errors, rather than building the component myself and using Claude to help when I got stuck. I've written a bit about my experience with this project [here]({{site.url}}/notes/2026/03/17/building-a-home-dashboard.html).

In Brittany Chiang's article, she refers to a change in her workflow from using AI as an "assistant" to letting AI become the "driver" and the user becoming the "navigator", and that's the change I've noticed in my own use of Claude for this home dashboard project when compared to previous projects. I wanted to display the temperature forecast graphically in a line to easily see at a glance if it would warm up or cool down throughout the week. I know I'm not reinventing the wheel with that idea, but in the past I would have been excited to sit down and figure out how to do that with code, and it would have taken me at least a few hours, probably much longer. But within seconds of suggesting it to Claude, Claude had written code that worked almost perfectly, with a few more prompts and edits. It was great to get that idea up and running almost immediately, but part of me was disappointed that I didn't take the time to figure it out myself.

Brittany Chiang made the analogy of reading a book versus listening to an audiobook. My analogy is that LLMs are like spoilers to a movie. The movie is the new, exciting problem you've discovered and want to figure out. With spoilers, you can try your best to avoid them. But with LLMs, it's becoming increasingly difficult to opt out. If your livelihood depends on knowing what happens in the movie, it's going to be difficult to willingly avoid spoilers. In an extremely competitive job market, especially for new grads, I'm competing with others who are likely also using AI to create projects at a rapid rate. If I opt out and decide to create, for example, a complicated app by hand for weeks that others can build in a day, I'm simply falling behind. I never intend on blindly building projects with AI without filtering and understanding the end result, but I am considering moving towards the "driver"/"navigator" workflow, rather than the "assistant" approach, or at least experimenting with it to understand how this might change the way we work in the future. But it isn't the workflow that I find most fulfilling, and I want to find a way to make sure my work continues to be fulfilling for me.

Another issue that I feel the need to briefly touch on is the ethics of using AI. Last year I built [Cadence](https://github.com/emoore29/cadence), a project that was fraught with its own development challenges. When I finally published a demo of the site, within the first 30 seconds GPTBot had retrieved my frontend code:

It was only then that I (I guess naively) discovered OpenAI had a webcrawler that would grab online content for training ChatGPT models, and that you had to opt out rather than opt in:

![GPTBot Info]({{site.url}}/assets/images/ai/gptbot.png)

There is something very different about writing a guide for other humans to read or making my repositories public for transparency and knowledge-sharing, to having your work taken to train an AI agent built by a company that has profit and shareholders as its primary concerns.

One thing I realised early on when I was copy-pasting code from StackOverflow or using open-source frameworks and libraries was that programming is inherently a collaborative process, and that it has largely been built on humans sharing their hard work and knowledge freely with the world. Now that vast amounts of that knowledge have been ingested into AI agents, that collaborative process seems to be disrupted. I think Hank Green had an interesting point [here](https://youtu.be/8MLbOulrLA0?si=5eOl0zel3pV_y_14&t=935) when he mentioned that communication between humans is one of the most significant parts of human society. If we remove that human-to-human communication and make it human-to-AI-to-human, what does that change?

Anyway, I think I've gone a bit beyond my original goal of this post. But I hope that sharing these thoughts will resonate with others or at the very least, provide some transparency around my own use of AI in my work and personal projects.
